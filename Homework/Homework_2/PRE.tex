	{\color{deepred}\fontspec{Georgia} by Zongyi Liu}
	
	
	\textit{Introduction}
	
	The paper I chose was titled \textit{Granular Credit Risk} written by four economists (Sigurd Galaasen, Rustam Jamilov, Ragnar Juelsrud, and H\'{e}l\`{e}ne Rey) from Norway. This paper studies how large individual loans (``granular exposures'') in bank portfolios can generate macroeconomic effects, challenging the traditional view that idiosyncratic credit risk disappears through diversification. It used detailed loan-level administrative data from Norway, and the result was that shocks to large borrowers can significantly affect banks and the broader economy.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Motivation}
	
	Traditional banking theory (e.g., Diamond 1984) argues that when banks hold many small loans, idiosyncratic borrower risk averages out through diversification. But in reality bank portfolios are highly concentrated, with a fat-tailed distribution of loan sizes, meaning a few large borrowers account for a substantial share of lending. The paper investigates whether shocks to such large borrowers can survive aggregation and influence bank outcomes and the real economy. This idea is connected to the ``granular hypothesis'' in macroeconomics (Gabaix 2011), which argues that shocks to large economic agents can generate aggregate fluctuations.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Data and Setup}
	
	The authors use a matched bank-firm dataset from Norway containing detailed information on bank lending relationships. Their empirical strategy exploits the fat-tailed distribution of loan shares and applies a granular instrumental variable (GIV) approach to isolate exogenous shocks to large borrowers. The identification strategy allows them to measure how shocks to specific borrowers propagate through banks and ultimately affect other firms.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Main Findings}
	
	The paper documents three key results.
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item \textit{Idiosyncratic borrower shocks affect bank outcomes.} Negative shocks to large borrowers reduce loan returns and affect banks' balance sheets. Because some exposures are large relative to the bank's portfolio, these shocks do not wash out through diversification.
		\item \textit{Granular credit risk survives portfolio aggregation.} Even though banks hold many loans, the presence of large exposures means that idiosyncratic shocks remain economically significant at the bank level.
		\item \textit{Credit shocks spill over to other firms and the macroeconomy.} Banks affected by granular shocks adjust their lending behavior, generating spillovers to other firms that were not directly exposed to the original shock. The authors find that these spillovers reduce firms' investment, increase default risk, and affect broader economic activity. Thus shocks originating from a few large borrowers can propagate through the financial system and have macroeconomic consequences.
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Implications}
	
	The results support the rationale behind large exposure regulations in banking (such as Basel rules limiting concentration risk). The authors estimate that reducing credit concentration beyond what is justified by firm size could reduce the macroeconomic pass-through of granular credit shocks by about 40\%, with further reductions possible through better portfolio diversification. Overall, the study demonstrates that micro-level borrower shocks can have macroeconomic consequences when financial exposures are concentrated, highlighting the importance of monitoring large credit exposures in banking systems.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Intro Section (tentative)}
	
	Accurate prediction of borrower default helps banks reduce potential losses and improve credit allocation decisions. 	\textit{Introduction}
	
	The models used to predict the default rate are various, while stakeholders can only see the prediction results instead of the procedures, leading to a lack of trust in the prediction model. The paper \textit{Explainable Prediction of Loan Default Based on Machine Learning Models} analyzed and compared four main prediction models: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, XGBoost, and LightGBM. The authors then leverage the LIME (Local Interpretable Model-Agnostic Explanations) technique to explain the model results and relevant factors, finding that prediction results are influenced by the amount, time, level of the loan, and the borrower's credit rating.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Data and Setup}
	
	The data used for this paper is provided by Datawhale, extracted from a competition website held by ALIYUN. The raw data contains 800,000 entries; the analysis selected 46 variables, including 15 anonymous variables ($n_0$ to $n_{14}$), covering all related variables centered around borrowers' financial condition, credit profile, and loan-related information, with \texttt{isDefault} as the dependent variable coded with binary indicators. For the data preprocessing phase, the authors filled all missing values with the median, deleted all outliers on a $3\sigma$ basis, and reduced the data entries to 612,742 for final modeling.
	
	For model training, the project performed an 80/20 train-test split, randomly assigning 80\% of the data for training and 20\% for testing. The authors also utilized five-fold cross-validation for the LightGBM model after rating it as the best-performing model, examining its stability with a classification threshold of 0.5.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Methodology}
	
	The project can be divided into two phases: model comparison and best-performing model explanation.
	
	For the model comparison stage, four classification models are evaluated: Logistic Regression as the traditional baseline examining linear relationships, Decision Tree as a classical tree model, and XGBoost and LightGBM as tree-based ensemble boosting models studying complicated nonlinear relationships. During data preprocessing, the authors utilized multiple techniques including data deletion, IV (information value) for variable filtering, PCA for highly correlated variables, and PSI to test stability.
	
	For model evaluation, the paper used AUC (area under the ROC curve), precision, accuracy, and KS (Kolmogorov--Smirnov) statistic to assess each model from different perspectives. To provide interpretability, the authors applied the LIME technique to the best-performing LightGBM model to explain how individual predictions were made.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Main Findings}
	
	All examined models performed strongly with an AUC larger than 0.7, with XGBoost and LightGBM slightly outperforming the two more traditional models (Logistic Regression and Decision Tree). LightGBM also demonstrated higher accuracy and precision compared to the other models across additional metrics, leading the authors to conclude that tree-based boosting models perform better for default prediction than traditional linear models, with LightGBM being the best among them.
	
	To further investigate model interpretability, five-fold cross-validation on LightGBM yielded an average AUC of 0.7206 and accuracy of 0.8086, along with small variance, demonstrating stability and a low level of overfitting. LIME identified several notable variables from the default sample, including loan term, loan grade, DTI (debt-to-income ratio), home ownership status, and credit score, which the authors identify as the key attributes affecting loan default risk.

	\subsection{Article 4}
	
	{\color{deepred}\fontspec{Georgia} by Jiaming Luo}

	
	\textit{Introduction}
	
	The paper I chose is titled \textit{Understanding the Performance of Machine Learning Models to Predict Credit Default: A Novel Approach for Supervisory Evaluation} by Andr\'{e}s Alonso and Jos\'{e} Manuel Carb\'{o}. The paper studies how different machine learning (ML) methods perform in credit default prediction using a unique, anonymized dataset from a major Spanish bank. The authors compare a traditional benchmark---logistic regression (Logit)---with a set of widely used ML models: Lasso-penalized logistic regression, CART, Random Forest, XGBoost, and Deep Neural Networks. Importantly, they evaluate models the way a regulator or supervisor would validate an Internal Ratings-Based (IRB) system, focusing on both classification (discriminating default vs.\ non-default) and calibration (whether predicted probabilities match observed default rates).
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Motivation}
	
	ML models are increasingly used in credit risk management---covering credit scoring, provisioning, monitoring, and regulatory capital calculation---but from a supervisory perspective they raise concerns around interpretability, stability, and governance. The paper argues that before supervisors can decide how to regulate ML, it is important to understand the size of the potential benefit in practical business terms. The authors frame a key tradeoff: ML can improve predictive performance, but may also increase ``supervisory cost'' (difficulty of evaluation and approval). They therefore aim to quantify not only predictive gains, but also the economic impact of improved prediction---especially in the form of regulatory capital savings under Basel IRB rules.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Data and Setup}
	
	The authors use an anonymized dataset from Banco Santander with more than 75,000 consumer credit operations. The dataset includes up to 370 risk factors per loan, but feature labels are intentionally not disclosed to preserve anonymization. The observed default rate is approximately 3.95\%. The data has no time dimension, so the study focuses on point-in-time default prediction rather than through-the-cycle modeling.
	
	Six models are trained and compared: Logit, Lasso-logit, CART, Random Forest, XGBoost, and Deep Neural Networks. The workflow uses standard validation practice: an 80/20 train-test split, out-of-sample evaluation, and standardized inputs (mean 0, variance 1). To separate ``information advantage'' (more data or features) from ``model advantage'' (algorithmic power), the authors run simulation exercises varying sample size and number of features, tracking how performance changes in each case.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Main Findings}
	
	The paper documents three key results.
	\begin{enumerate}
		\item \textit{ML models outperform traditional Logit in classification.} Using AUC-ROC as the main discrimination metric, Logit performs worst and advanced ML models perform better, with XGBoost and Random Forest ranking best. The reported AUCs are approximately: Logit 0.79, Lasso 0.80, CART 0.81, Deep Neural Network 0.81, Random Forest 0.83, and XGBoost 0.84.
		\item \textit{Calibration matters, and ML improves calibration.} The paper uses the Brier score and reliability curves to assess calibration. Differences in Brier score are numerically small due to the low default rate, but XGBoost and Random Forest again perform best. Traditional models such as Logit and Lasso can systematically under- or over-estimate the probability of default (PD) in certain probability ranges, while XGBoost and Random Forest stay closer to the 45-degree perfect calibration line.
		\item \textit{More complex algorithms do not automatically predict better.} Deep Neural Networks are not consistently superior to tree-based ensembles. In both discrimination and calibration, tree-based methods often win despite neural networks being architecturally more complex.
	\end{enumerate}
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Intro Section (tentative)}
	
	Credit default prediction is a core component of modern credit risk management because it supports decisions about loan approval, pricing, provisioning, and capital planning. Recent work emphasizes that model quality should be evaluated not only by how well a model ranks borrowers by risk, but also by how accurately it estimates default probabilities, since PD is directly used in risk systems such as Basel's IRB framework. Alonso and Carb\'{o} study this issue by comparing traditional logistic regression with modern machine learning approaches---including Lasso-logit, CART, Random Forest, XGBoost, and Deep Neural Networks---using an anonymized dataset of more than 75,000 consumer loans from a major Spanish bank. Their results show that ML models typically outperform Logit in both discrimination and calibration, but that more complex algorithms do not necessarily predict better: tree-based ensemble methods, especially XGBoost and Random Forest, achieve the strongest overall performance. Importantly, the authors translate predictive gains into business impact by showing that improved PD estimation can lead to meaningful reductions in regulatory capital requirements under IRB-style calculations. Motivated by this literature, our project analyzes a public credit risk dataset and formulates default prediction as a binary classification problem using borrower and loan characteristics as predictors. We aim to compare baseline statistical models with machine learning methods, evaluate both discrimination and calibration performance, and identify the most important drivers of default risk, with the broader goal of building a model that is both accurate and practically useful for credit risk assessment and decision-making.

	\subsection{Article 5}
	
	{\color{deepred}\fontspec{Georgia} by Keyan Su}
	
	
	\textit{Introduction}
	
	This paper provides a comprehensive update to the influential 2003 benchmarking study by Baesens et al.\ on classification algorithms for credit scoring.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Methodology}
	
	The study compares 41 classification algorithms across eight real-world retail credit scoring datasets. The classifiers span three categories: individual classifiers, homogeneous ensembles, and heterogeneous ensembles that combine multiple algorithm types. Using $N\times 2$ cross-validation, the authors evaluate performance using six measures. Non-parametric statistical tests (Friedman test with post-hoc procedures) ensure robust comparisons.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Key Findings}
	
	Heterogeneous ensemble classifiers dominate the top rankings, securing the first 11 positions overall. The best-performing classifier is HCES-Bag, followed by weighted averaging ensembles. Random forest emerges as the strongest homogeneous ensemble (ranked 12th), while logistic regression---the industry standard---ranks only 24th.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Financial Implications}
	
	The authors use a cost-sensitive analysis with varying misclassification cost ratios and find that advanced classifiers can reduce error costs by 3--6\% compared to logistic regression. However, the most statistically accurate classifier (HCES-Bag) does not always yield the highest cost savings, particularly when the cost of misclassifying bad risks is very high.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Performance Measure Analysis}
	
	Correlation analysis reveals that AUC, H-measure, PCC, and KS produce highly similar classifier rankings. However, the Brier Score and partial Gini index capture distinct performance aspects, leading the authors to recommend using at least three measures: AUC (or H-measure), partial Gini, and Brier Score.
	
	\medskip
	
	\textit{Conclusions}
	
	The authors recommend random forest as a minimum benchmark for future studies and caution against using logistic regression as the sole comparator. Research attention might usefully shift toward data quality, variable selection, and LGD/EAD modeling.

		\subsection{Part III}
		Under $H_0$, the combined sample of size $m+n$ comes from the same continuous distribution, so the ranks $1,2,\dots,m+n$ are randomly assigned to the observations. Let $R_i$ denote the rank of $X_i$. Then $T_1=\sum_{i=1}^m R_i$.
		
		\medskip
		\noindent\emph{Expected Value.}
		By the hint, each $R_i$ is equally likely to be any value in $\{1,2,\dots,m+n\}$, so $E[R_i]=\frac{1+2+\cdots+(m+n)}{m+n}=\frac{m+n+1}{2}$. Therefore, by linearity of expectation,
		$$E[T_1]=E\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m R_i\right]=\sum_{i=1}^m E[R_i]=m\cdot\frac{m+n+1}{2}=\frac{m(m+n+1)}{2}.$$
		
		\medskip
		\noindent\emph{Variance.}
		Write $N=m+n$. Then
		$\operatorname{Var}(T_1)=\sum_{i=1}^m \operatorname{Var}(R_i)+2\sum_{1\le i<j\le m}\operatorname{Cov}(R_i,R_j).$ Since $R_i$ is uniform on $\{1,\dots,N\}$, we have $E[R_i]=\frac{N+1}{2}$ and $E[R_i^2]=\frac{1^2+2^2+\cdots+N^2}{N}=\frac{(N+1)(2N+1)}{6}$, so
		$$\operatorname{Var}(R_i)=E[R_i^2]-(E[R_i])^2=\frac{(N+1)(2N+1)}{6}-\frac{(N+1)^2}{4}=\frac{N^2-1}{12}.$$
		
		For $i\neq j$, $(R_i,R_j)$ is a uniformly random ordered pair of distinct values from $\{1,\dots,N\}$, so there are $N(N-1)$ equally likely outcomes. Using the identity
		$$\sum_{\substack{a,b=1\\a\neq b}}^{N}ab=\left(\sum_{a=1}^N a\right)^{\!2}-\sum_{a=1}^N a^2=\frac{N^2(N+1)^2}{4}-\frac{N(N+1)(2N+1)}{6},$$
		we compute
		\begin{align*}
			E[R_iR_j]
			&=\frac{1}{N(N-1)}\left[\frac{N^2(N+1)^2}{4}-\frac{N(N+1)(2N+1)}{6}\right]
			=\frac{N(N+1)}{N(N-1)}\left[\frac{N(N+1)}{4}-\frac{2N+1}{6}\right]\\
			&=\frac{N+1}{N-1}\cdot\frac{3N(N+1)-2(2N+1)}{12}
			=\frac{N+1}{N-1}\cdot\frac{3N^2-N-2}{12}
			=\frac{N+1}{N-1}\cdot\frac{(N-1)(3N+2)}{12}
			=\frac{(N+1)(3N+2)}{12}.
		\end{align*}
		Hence
		\begin{align*}
			\operatorname{Cov}(R_i,R_j)
			&=E[R_iR_j]-E[R_i]\,E[R_j]
			=\frac{(N+1)(3N+2)}{12}-\left(\frac{N+1}{2}\right)^{\!2}
			=(N+1)\left[\frac{3N+2}{12}-\frac{N+1}{4}\right]
			=(N+1)\cdot\frac{-1}{12}
		\end{align*} =$-\frac{N+1}{12}.$ Then substituting back and using $2\binom{m}{2}=m(m-1)$, we have
		$\operatorname{Var}(T_1)=m\cdot\frac{N^2-1}{12}+m(m-1)\!\left(-\frac{N+1}{12}\right)=\frac{m(N^2-1)-m(m-1)(N+1)}{12}=\frac{m(N+1)\bigl[(N-1)-(m-1)\bigr]}{12}=\frac{m(N+1)(N-m)}{12}.$
		
		Since $N=m+n$ gives $N-m=n$, we conclude that
		$$E[T_1]=\frac{m(m+n+1)}{2},  \operatorname{Var}(T_1)=\frac{mn(m+n+1)}{12}$$

		\section{Question 5}
		{\color{deepred}\fontspec{Georgia} Two-Sample Pooled Test and Likelihood Ratio}
		
		Recall the pooled two-sample t-procedure, which assumes the model:
		
		\medskip
		
		\underline{Assumptions}
		
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_m$ is a random sample from a normal distribution with mean $\mu_1$ and variance $\sigma^2$. The variance $\sigma^2$ is unknown.
			
			\item $Y_1, Y_2, \ldots, Y_n$ is a random sample from a normal distribution with mean $\mu_2$ and variance $\sigma^2$. The variance $\sigma^2$ is unknown.
			
			\item The $X$ and $Y$ samples are independent of one another.
		\end{enumerate}
		
		\medskip
		
		Consider the two tailed hypothesis
		\[
		H_0 : \mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0 
		\text{versus} 
		H_A : \mu_1 - \mu_2 \neq 0 .
		\]
		
		\medskip
		
		\emph{Perform the following tasks}
		
		\begin{enumerate}
			
			\item[i] Derive an expression for the likelihood
			\[
			\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}(\mu_1,\mu_2,\sigma^2).
			\]
			
			\item[ii] Find the maximum likelihood estimator of
			\[
			\theta = (\mu_1,\mu_2,\sigma^2).
			\]
			
			Note that you are not required to perform the \emph{second derivative test} for this problem.
			
			\item[iii] Derive the likelihood ratio test statistic $\lambda$. Note that you are not required to perform the \emph{second derivative test} for this problem.
			
			\item[iv] Show that the likelihood ratio test can be expressed in terms of the t-statistic $(T)$, i.e., there exists a function $f$ such that
			\[
			\lambda = f(T).
			\]
			
		\end{enumerate}
		
		\textbf{Answer}
		
		\subsection{Part I}
		
		Let $x_1,\dots,x_m$ be the observed $X$-sample and $y_1,\dots,y_n$ the observed $Y$-sample. Under the model,
		$
		X_i \stackrel{iid}{\sim} N(\mu_1,\sigma^2), 
		Y_j \stackrel{iid}{\sim} N(\mu_2,\sigma^2),
		$
		with the two samples independent.
		
		Define
		$
		\bar X=\frac1m\sum_{i=1}^m X_i, 
		\bar Y=\frac1n\sum_{j=1}^n Y_j,
		$
		and let
		$
		SSE_1=\sum_{i=1}^m (X_i-\bar X)^2, 
		SSE_2=\sum_{j=1}^n (Y_j-\bar Y)^2.
		$
		
		
		By independence,
		$
		\mathcal L(\mu_1,\mu_2,\sigma^2)
		=
		\prod_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
		\exp\!\left(-\frac{(x_i-\mu_1)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)
		\prod_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}}
		\exp\!\left(-\frac{(y_j-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).
		$
		Thus
		$
		\mathcal L(\mu_1,\mu_2,\sigma^2)
		=
		(2\pi\sigma^2)^{-(m+n)/2}
		\exp\!\left(
		-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
		\left[
		\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\mu_1)^2+\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j-\mu_2)^2
		\right]
		\right).
		$
		Equivalently, the log-likelihood is
		$
		\ell(\mu_1,\mu_2,\sigma^2)
		=
		-\frac{m+n}{2}\log(2\pi)
		-\frac{m+n}{2}\log(\sigma^2)
		-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
		\left[
		\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\mu_1)^2+\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j-\mu_2)^2
		\right].
		$
		
		\clearpage
		
		\subsection{Part II}
		
		To maximize $\mathcal L$, equivalently maximize $\ell$. Differentiating with respect to $\mu_1$ and $\mu_2$ gives
		$
		\hat\mu_1=\bar X,  \hat\mu_2=\bar Y.
		$ Substituting these into the log-likelihood, we maximize in $\sigma^2$:
		$$
		\ell(\bar X,\bar Y,\sigma^2)
		=
		-\frac{m+n}{2}\log(2\pi)
		-\frac{m+n}{2}\log(\sigma^2)
		-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
		\left[
		\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\bar X)^2+\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j-\bar Y)^2
		\right].
		$$
		Hence
		$
		\hat\sigma^2
		=
		\frac{1}{m+n}
		\left[
		\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\bar X)^2+\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j-\bar Y)^2
		\right]
		=
		\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}{m+n}.
		$ Therefore the unrestricted MLE is
		$
		\hat\theta=
		\left(
		\bar X,\bar Y,\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}{m+n}
		\right).
		$
		
		\clearpage
		
		\subsection{Part III}
		
		Under $H_0:\mu_1-\mu_2=0$, we write $\mu_1=\mu_2=\mu$. Then the likelihood becomes $
		\mathcal L_0(\mu,\sigma^2)$\\
		$
		=
		(2\pi\sigma^2)^{-(m+n)/2}
		\exp\!\left(
		-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}
		\left[
		\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\mu)^2+\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j-\mu)^2
		\right]
		\right).
		$ The MLE of $\mu$ under $H_0$ is the combined sample mean
		$
		\hat\mu_0=\bar Z=\frac{m\bar X+n\bar Y}{m+n}.
		$
		
		The constrained MLE of $\sigma^2$ is
		$
		\hat\sigma_0^2
		=
		\frac{1}{m+n}
		\left[
		\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\bar Z)^2+\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j-\bar Z)^2
		\right].
		$ Using the identity
		$
		\sum_{i=1}^m (x_i-\bar Z)^2+\sum_{j=1}^n (y_j-\bar Z)^2$$
		=
		SSE_1+SSE_2+\frac{mn}{m+n}(\bar X-\bar Y)^2,
		$
		we get
		$
		\hat\sigma_0^2
		=
		\frac{1}{m+n}
		\left[
		SSE_1+SSE_2+\frac{mn}{m+n}(\bar X-\bar Y)^2
		\right].
		$
		
		The likelihood ratio statistic is
		$
		\lambda
		=
		\frac{\sup_{H_0}\mathcal L}{\sup_{\Theta}\mathcal L}.
		$
		Since at the maximizers the exponential term becomes $e^{-(m+n)/2}$ in both numerator and denominator, we obtain
		$
		\lambda
		=
		\left(\frac{\hat\sigma^2}{\hat\sigma_0^2}\right)^{(m+n)/2}.
		$
		Thus
		$
		\lambda
		=
		\left(
		\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}
		{SSE_1+SSE_2+\frac{mn}{m+n}(\bar X-\bar Y)^2}
		\right)^{(m+n)/2}.
		$
		
		\clearpage
		
		\subsection{Part IV}
		
		Recall the pooled variance
		$
		S_p^2=\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}{m+n-2},
		$
		and the pooled two-sample $t$-statistic
		$
		T
		=
		\frac{\bar X-\bar Y}
		{S_p\sqrt{\frac1m+\frac1n}}.
		$
		Then
		$
		T^2
		=
		\frac{(\bar X-\bar Y)^2}
		{S_p^2\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)}
		=
		\frac{(\bar X-\bar Y)^2}
		{\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}{m+n-2}\cdot \frac{m+n}{mn}}
		=
		\frac{mn}{m+n}\cdot \frac{(m+n-2)(\bar X-\bar Y)^2}{SSE_1+SSE_2}.
		$
		Hence
		$
		\frac{mn}{m+n}(\bar X-\bar Y)^2
		=
		\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}{m+n-2}\,T^2.
		$
		
		Substitute this into the formula for $\lambda$:
		$
		\lambda
		=
		\left(
		\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}
		{SSE_1+SSE_2+\frac{SSE_1+SSE_2}{m+n-2}T^2}
		\right)^{(m+n)/2}.
		$
		Factor out $SSE_1+SSE_2$ from the denominator:
		$
		\lambda
		=
		\left(
		\frac{1}
		{1+\frac{T^2}{m+n-2}}
		\right)^{(m+n)/2}.
		$
		
		Therefore $\lambda=f(T)$ with
		$
		f(t)=\left(1+\frac{t^2}{m+n-2}\right)^{-(m+n)/2}.
		$
		
		So the likelihood ratio test is an equivalent test to the pooled two-sample $t$-test, since $\lambda$ is a monotone decreasing function of $|T|$.
		
		
		
		
		\clearpage
		
		
		\section{Question 6}
		
		{\color{deepred}\fontspec{Georgia} Parametric and Non-parametric Paired Procedures}
		
		Consider testing the stopping distance [ft] of two types of tires on 6 different cars. 
		The response variable is the stopping distance of the car after the brakes are applied 
		at 60 mph. The two types of tires are: Winter and High Performance. 
		Does the data set indicate that the stopping distance differs between the two tire types?
		
		\medskip
		
		\begin{center}
			\begin{tabular}{ccc}
				\hline
				Car & High Performance & Winter \\
				\hline
				1 & 226.6 & 239.5 \\
				2 & 230.5 & 257.5 \\
				3 & 220.0 & 236.8 \\
				4 & 215.9 & 223.9 \\
				5 & 231.5 & 248.0 \\
				6 & 235.5 & 253.6 \\
				\hline
			\end{tabular}
		\end{center}
		
		\medskip
		
		\emph{Perform the following tasks}
		
		\begin{enumerate}
			
			\item[i] Assuming the stopping distance for both tire types are approximately normally distributed, run the paired two-sample t-procedure to test the claim
			
			\[
			H_0 : \mu_D = 0 
			\text{ versus } 
			H_A : \mu_D \neq 0 .
			\]
			
			Make sure to include all relevant steps in the testing procedure for full credit.
			
			\item[ii] Assuming the stopping distance for $D = X - Y$ is symmetric, run the Wilcoxon signed-rank procedure to test the claim
			
			\[
			H_0 : \mu_D = 0 
			\text{ versus } 
			H_A : \mu_D \neq 0 .
			\]
			
			Make sure to include all relevant steps in the testing procedure for full credit.
			
		\end{enumerate}
		
		
		\textbf{Answer}
		
		
		
		
		\subsection{Part I}
		Let $X$ denote the stopping distance for High Performance tires and $Y$ the stopping distance for Winter tires. Define paired differences
		$
		D_i = X_i - Y_i,  i=1,\dots,6.
		$
		From the data we obtain
		$
		D = (-12.9,-27.0,-16.8,-8.0,-16.5,-18.1).
		$
		
		We test
		$
		H_0:\mu_D=0
		\text{vs} 
		H_A:\mu_D\ne 0.
		$ First compute the sample mean
		$
		\bar D=\frac{1}{6}\sum_{i=1}^6 D_i
		= -16.55.
		$ Next compute the sample standard deviation
		$
		s_D \approx 6.49.
		$ The test statistic is
		$
		t=\frac{\bar D-0}{s_D/\sqrt{n}}
		=\frac{-16.55}{6.49/\sqrt{6}}
		\approx -6.25.
		$ The degrees of freedom are
		$
		df=n-1=5.
		$
		
		For a two–sided test, the critical value at $\alpha=0.05$ is approximately
		$
		t_{0.025,5}\approx 2.571.$ Since $|t|=6.25>2.571$, we reject $H_0$. The $p$–value is very small ($p<0.01$).
		
		There is strong evidence that the mean stopping distance differs between the two tire types. Because $\bar D<0$, High Performance tires tend to have shorter stopping distances than Winter tires.
		
		\clearpage
		
		\subsection{Part II}
		
		Again test $H_0:\mu_D=0$ vs $H_A:\mu_D\ne 0$.
		
		Compute the absolute differences $|D|=(12.9,27.0,16.8,8.0,16.5,18.1)$ and rank them from smallest to largest:
		
		\begin{center}
			\begin{tabular}{ccc}
				\hline
				$D_i$ & $|D_i|$ & Rank \\
				\hline
				$-8.0$  & 8.0  & 1 \\
				$-12.9$ & 12.9 & 2 \\
				$-16.5$ & 16.5 & 3 \\
				$-16.8$ & 16.8 & 4 \\
				$-18.1$ & 18.1 & 5 \\
				$-27.0$ & 27.0 & 6 \\
				\hline
			\end{tabular}
		\end{center}
		
		Since all differences are negative, the positive and negative rank sums are
		$$W_+ = 0, \qquad W_- = 1+2+3+4+5+6 = 21.$$
		The Wilcoxon signed-rank statistic is $W = \min(W_+, W_-) = 0$.
		
		To compute the exact two-sided $p$-value, note that under $H_0$ each of the $2^6 = 64$ sign assignments is equally likely. The value $W_+ = 0$ occurs only when all six differences are assigned a negative sign, which corresponds to exactly $1$ out of $64$ arrangements. For a two-sided test we double this probability, giving
		$$p = \frac{2 \times 1}{2^6} = \frac{2}{64} = 0.03125.$$
		Since $p = 0.03125 < 0.05$, we reject $H_0$ and conclude that the stopping distance differs significantly between the two tire types.
		\clearpage
		
		\section{Question 7}
		
		{\color{deepred}\fontspec{Georgia} Categorical Hypothesis Testing}
		
		\medskip
		
		Binomial data gathered from more than one population are often presented in a contingency table. 
		For the case of two populations, the table might look like
		
		\begin{center}
			\begin{tabular}{c|c|c|c}
				& Population 1 & Population 2 & Total \\
				\hline
				Successes & $S_1$ & $S_2$ & $S = S_1 + S_2$ \\
				Failures & $F_1$ & $F_2$ & $F = F_1 + F_2$ \\
				\hline
				Total & $m$ & $n$ & $N = m + n$
			\end{tabular}
		\end{center}
		
		where Population 1 is $\text{Binomial}(m,p_1)$, with $S_1$ successes and $F_1$ failures, and Population 2 is $\text{Binomial}(n,p_2)$, with $S_2$ successes and $F_2$ failures. 
		We will motivate both the two-sample $z$-test for proportions and the chi-squared test for association based on the $2\times2$ contingency table. 
		The hypothesis of interest is:
		
		\[
		H_0: p_1 = p_2
		\text{versus} 
		H_A: p_1 \neq p_2
		\]
		
		\emph{Perform the following tasks}
		
		\begin{enumerate}
			
			\item[i] Show that a test can be based on the statistic
			\[
			W = \frac{(\hat p_1 - \hat p_2)^2}{\left(\frac{1}{m} + \frac{1}{n}\right)\left(\hat p (1-\hat p)\right)},
			\]
			where $\hat p_1 = S_1/m$, $\hat p_2 = S_2/n$, and
			\[
			\hat p = \frac{S_1 + S_2}{m+n}.
			\]
			Also show that as $m,n \to \infty$, the distribution of $W$ approaches $\chi^2_1$.
			
			\item[ii] Another way of measuring the departure from $H_0$ is by calculating the expected frequency table. 
			This table is constructed by conditioning on the marginal totals and filling in the table according to $H_0: p_1=p_2$, that is,
			
			\medskip
			
			\begin{center}
				Expected Frequencies
				
				\begin{tabular}{c|c|c|c}
					& Population 1 & Population 2 & Total \\
					\hline
					Successes & $\frac{mS}{m+n}$ & $\frac{nS}{m+n}$ & $S = S_1 + S_2$ \\
					Failures & $\frac{mF}{m+n}$ & $\frac{nF}{m+n}$ & $F = F_1 + F_2$ \\
					\hline
					Total & $m$ & $n$ & $N = m+n$
				\end{tabular}
			\end{center}
			
			Using the expected frequency table, a statistic $W^*$ is computed by going through the cells of the tables and computing
			
			\[
			W^* =
			\sum \frac{(\text{observed} - \text{expected})^2}{\text{expected}}
			\]
			
			\[
			=
			\frac{\left(S_1 - \frac{mS}{m+n}\right)^2}{\frac{mS}{m+n}}
			+
			\frac{\left(S_2 - \frac{nS}{m+n}\right)^2}{\frac{nS}{m+n}}
			+
			\frac{\left(F_1 - \frac{mF}{m+n}\right)^2}{\frac{mF}{m+n}}
			+
			\frac{\left(F_2 - \frac{nF}{m+n}\right)^2}{\frac{nF}{m+n}}.
			\]
			
			Show, algebraically, that $W = W^*$ and hence that $W^*$ is asymptotically chi-squared.
			
			\item[iii] A famous medical experiment was conducted by Joseph Lister in the late 1800s. 
			Mortality associated with surgery was quite high, and Lister conjectured that the use of disinfectant, carbolic acid, would help. 
			Over a period of several years Lister performed 75 amputations with and without carbonic acid. 
			The data are given here:
			
			\begin{center}
				Carbolic acid Used?
				
				\begin{tabular}{c|c|c}
					& Yes & No \\
					\hline
					Patient lived & 34 & 19 \\
					Patient died & 6 & 16
				\end{tabular}
			\end{center}
			
			Use these data to test whether the use of carbolic acid is associated with patient mortality.
			
		\end{enumerate}
		
		
		\textbf{Answer}
		
		
		\subsection{Part I}
		Under $H_0:p_1=p_2=:p$, we have $\hat p_1=S_1/m$ and $\hat p_2=S_2/n$ with
		$\mathbb E(\hat p_1-\hat p_2)=0$ and
		$\mathrm{Var}(\hat p_1-\hat p_2)=p(1-p)\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)$.
		By the central limit theorem,
		$
		\frac{\hat p_1-\hat p_2}
		{\sqrt{p(1-p)\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)}}
		\Rightarrow N(0,1)
		$
		as $m,n\to\infty$.
		Replacing $p$ by the pooled estimator
		$
		\hat p=\frac{S_1+S_2}{m+n},
		$
		the large-sample statistic becomes
		$
		Z=\frac{\hat p_1-\hat p_2}
		{\sqrt{\hat p(1-\hat p)\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)}}.
		$
		Hence
		$
		W=Z^2=\frac{(\hat p_1-\hat p_2)^2}
		{\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)\hat p(1-\hat p)}.
		$
		Since $Z\Rightarrow N(0,1)$, it follows that $W\Rightarrow\chi^2_1$.
		
		\subsection{Part II}
		
		Define $N=m+n$, $S=S_1+S_2$, and $F=F_1+F_2$.
		Let $A=S_1-\frac{mS}{N}$. Then
		$
		S_2-\frac{nS}{N}=-A,
		$
		$
		F_1-\frac{mF}{N}=-A,
		$
		$
		F_2-\frac{nF}{N}=A.
		$
		Hence
		$
		W^*=A^2\!\left(\frac{N}{mS}+\frac{N}{nS}+\frac{N}{mF}+\frac{N}{nF}\right)
		= A^2N\!\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)\!\left(\frac1S+\frac1F\right).
		$
		Since $\frac1S+\frac1F=\frac{N}{SF}$,
		$
		W^*=A^2\frac{N^2}{SF}\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right).
		$
		Now
		$
		A=S_1-\frac{mS}{N}=\frac{mS_2-nS_1}{N},
		$
		so
		$
		A^2=\frac{(mS_2-nS_1)^2}{N^2}.
		$
		Therefore
		$
		W^*=\frac{(mS_2-nS_1)^2}{SF}\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right).
		$
		Also
		$
		\hat p_1-\hat p_2=\frac{S_1}{m}-\frac{S_2}{n}
		=\frac{nS_1-mS_2}{mn}.
		$
		Since $\hat p(1-\hat p)=\frac{SF}{N^2}$ and
		$\frac1m+\frac1n=\frac{N}{mn}$, substituting into
		$
		W=\frac{(\hat p_1-\hat p_2)^2}
		{\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)\hat p(1-\hat p)}
		$
		gives
		$
		W=\frac{(mS_2-nS_1)^2}{SF}\left(\frac1m+\frac1n\right)=W^*.
		$
		Thus $W^*$ is also asymptotically $\chi^2_1$.
		
		\subsection{Part III}
		From the table we take “success’’ to mean death, giving
		$m=40$, $n=35$, $S_1=6$, $S_2=16$.
		Then $\hat p_1=6/40=0.15$, $\hat p_2=16/35\approx0.4571$, and
		$\hat p=22/75\approx0.2933$.
		The statistic is
		$
		W=\frac{(\hat p_1-\hat p_2)^2}
		{\left(\frac1{40}+\frac1{35}\right)\hat p(1-\hat p)}
		\approx 8.49.
		$
		Under $H_0$, $W\sim\chi^2_1$ asymptotically, giving
		$p\approx0.0036$.
		We therefore reject $H_0$ and conclude that use of carbolic acid is
		associated with mortality; the mortality rate is about $15\%$ with
		carbolic acid versus $45.7\%$ without it.

		\section{Question 8}
		
		{\color{deepred}\fontspec{Georgia} Goodness-of-fit}
		
		\medskip
		
		Construct a Chi-squared goodness-of-fit test to assess whether or not a dataset $(X)$ is normally distributed, i.e., test the null alternative pair:
		
		\[
		H_0 : X \sim \text{Normal}
		\text{versus} 
		H_A : X \text{ is not Normal}.
		\]
		
		To accomplish this task, you must first convert the continuous observed data into a frequency table, i.e., the heights of the bars on a histogram. 
		Then apply the goodness-of-fit test based on the observed and expected counts assuming the null hypothesis $H_0: X\sim\text{Normal}$ is true.
		
		Some considerations follow:
		
		\begin{itemize}
			
			\item Let $K$ be the number of \emph{bins} in the histogram. Note that changing the number of bins will consequently change the degrees of freedom of the chi-squared distribution.
			
			\item Don't forget that you have to estimate both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ in order to compute the expected counts, which also impacts the degrees of freedom of the chi-squared distribution.
			
			\item If you are successful in solving this problem, maybe simulate a few normal and non-normal datasets and compare the qqplots with the goodness-of-fit outcomes of the simulated data.
			
		\end{itemize}
		
		\textbf{Answer}
		
		We construct a Pearson chi-squared goodness-of-fit test for
		$$H_0: X\sim \text{Normal} \quad \text{versus} \quad H_A: X \text{ is not Normal}.$$
		Suppose we observe an i.i.d.\ sample $X_1,\dots,X_n$ from the distribution of $X$.
		
		\begin{enumerate}
			\item \textit{Estimate the normal parameters.}
			Under $H_0$, the data should come from some normal distribution $N(\mu,\sigma^2)$, where both $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ are unknown. We estimate them by
			$$\hat\mu=\bar X=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i, \qquad \hat\sigma^2=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i-\bar X)^2$$
			(or one may use the unbiased variance estimator; asymptotically this makes no difference for the test).
			
			\item \textit{Partition the real line into $K$ bins.}
			Choose cutpoints $-\infty=a_0<a_1<\cdots<a_{K-1}<a_K=\infty$ and define the bins $B_j=(a_{j-1},a_j]$ for $j=1,\dots,K$. Let the observed count in bin $j$ be
			$$O_j=\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{\{X_i\in B_j\}}, \qquad \text{so that} \qquad \sum_{j=1}^K O_j=n.$$
			A common practical choice is to select the bins so that, under $H_0$, the expected counts are roughly equal; for example, one may take $a_j$ to be the estimated normal quantiles satisfying
			$$\mathbb{P}_{\hat\mu,\hat\sigma}(X\le a_j)=\frac{j}{K},$$
			so that each expected count is about $n/K$.
			
			\item \textit{Compute the expected counts under $H_0$.}
			Under the fitted normal model $N(\hat\mu,\hat\sigma^2)$, the probability of bin $j$ is
			$$p_j=\mathbb{P}_{\hat\mu,\hat\sigma}(X\in B_j)=\Phi\!\left(\frac{a_j-\hat\mu}{\hat\sigma}\right)-\Phi\!\left(\frac{a_{j-1}-\hat\mu}{\hat\sigma}\right),$$
			where $\Phi$ is the standard normal cdf. Hence the expected count in bin $j$ is $E_j=np_j$. For the chi-squared approximation to be reliable, one usually requires all expected counts to be at least about $5$; if some $E_j$ are too small, adjacent bins should be merged.
			
			\item \textit{Form the chi-squared statistic.}
			The Pearson statistic is
			$$Q=\sum_{j=1}^K \frac{(O_j-E_j)^2}{E_j}.$$
			
			\item \textit{Determine the degrees of freedom.}
			If the bin probabilities were fully specified in advance, the statistic would have approximately a $\chi^2_{K-1}$ distribution, since the counts satisfy one constraint $\sum_{j=1}^K O_j=n$. However, since we estimate two parameters $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$ from the data, the degrees of freedom are reduced by $2$. Therefore, under $H_0$,
			$$Q \approx \chi^2_{K-3},$$
			and the test requires $K\ge 4$.
			
			\item \textit{Decision rule.}
			At significance level $\alpha$, reject $H_0$ if $Q>\chi^2_{K-3,\,1-\alpha}$, the $(1-\alpha)$-quantile of the $\chi^2_{K-3}$ distribution. Equivalently, the $p$-value is
			$$p\text{-value}=\mathbb{P}\!\left(\chi^2_{K-3}\ge Q_{\mathrm{obs}}\right),$$
			and we reject when this is less than $\alpha$.
		\end{enumerate}
		
		To compare this test with Q-Q plots, one could simulate:
		\begin{itemize}
			\item Normal data, e.g.\ $X_i\sim N(0,1)$;
			\item Heavy-tailed data, e.g.\ $X_i\sim t_3$;
			\item Skewed data, e.g.\ $X_i\sim \mathrm{Exp}(1)$;
			\item Mixture data, e.g.\ $X_i\sim 0.8\,N(0,1)+0.2\,N(3,1)$.
		\end{itemize}
		For each dataset, draw the Q-Q plot and compute the statistic $Q$. Typically, normal samples produce roughly linear Q-Q plots and non-significant chi-squared tests, while non-normal samples show curvature or tail deviations in the Q-Q plot and tend to produce significant goodness-of-fit statistics.
